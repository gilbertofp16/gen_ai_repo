# Sample Reviews

These examples show how the negotiation-power skill should review and improve user messages.

Use them as behaviour examples, not as fixed templates.

The skill should diagnose the text through:
1. Negotiation lens
2. Power lens
3. Main issue
4. Improved version
5. Why it is stronger
6. Optional stronger version when useful

---

# Example 1: Asking for portfolio feedback

## User text

Hi, sorry to bother you. I just wanted to ask if maybe you had time to review my portfolio. I know you are busy, but I would really appreciate any feedback. No worries if not.

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
The ask is low pressure, but it gives the other person too many easy exits. It does not make the request specific enough, so replying feels like work.

**Power lens:**  
The message sounds too apologetic and low-status. The user is asking like a burden instead of positioning the request as a focused, professional exchange.

**Main issue:**  
Too much apology and not enough clarity.

## Improved version

Hi [Name], I’d value your perspective on my portfolio, especially the positioning for senior product design roles. To make it easy, could you look at one section and tell me whether the story feels clear and credible?

Would you be opposed to sharing one specific suggestion this week?

## Why this is stronger

- Removes unnecessary apology.
- Makes the ask specific.
- Uses a no-oriented question.
- Reduces effort for the other person.
- Positions the user as serious and focused.

## Stronger option

Hi [Name], I’m refining my portfolio for senior product design roles and want to pressure-test the positioning. Could you review one section and tell me whether it clearly communicates seniority, impact, and product judgement?

Would you be opposed to sharing one specific suggestion this week?

# Example 2: Following up with a recruiter

## User text

Hi, just checking if there is any update. Sorry to chase, I know you are probably busy.

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
The message asks for an update but does not create momentum or define the next step.

**Power lens:**  
“Just checking” and “sorry to chase” weaken the user’s position. The user sounds like they are asking permission to follow up.

**Main issue:**  
The message is too passive.

## Improved version

Hi [Name], I’m following up on the role we discussed. Do you have an update on the next step or expected timeline?

## Why this is stronger

- Removes weak apology.
- Makes the ask clear.
- Keeps the tone professional.
- Positions the user as organised, not needy.

## Stronger option

Hi [Name], I’m following up on the role we discussed. Could you confirm whether I’m still being considered and what the next step looks like?

# Example 3: Challenging a project timeline

## User text

I’m not sure, but maybe this timeline is a bit too tight. I think we might need more time, but happy to do whatever the team thinks.

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
The user has a real concern but presents it too softly. There is no calibrated question to make the team confront the trade-off.

**Power lens:**  
The message gives away authority. “Happy to do whatever the team thinks” removes the user’s judgement from the conversation.

**Main issue:**  
The user is hiding their professional recommendation.

## Improved version

I recommend we revisit the timeline before committing. Based on the current scope, the main risk is that we move fast now but create rework later.

What would need to change for this timeline to be realistic without compromising quality?

## Why this is stronger

- Replaces hesitation with a recommendation.
- Shows business risk.
- Uses a calibrated question.
- Makes the trade-off visible.
- Positions the user as someone protecting quality and delivery.

## Stronger option

I do not recommend committing to this timeline without adjusting scope. The risk is not only delivery pressure, it is rework and quality loss later.

If the deadline is fixed, what scope are we willing to reduce?

# Example 4: Asking for a raise

## User text

I wanted to ask if it would be possible to discuss my salary. I know budgets are difficult, but I’ve been working hard and I think maybe it could be reviewed.

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
The user is asking for a raise without enough evidence, anchor, or framing. They mention the company’s objection before stating their own value.

**Power lens:**  
The message is too tentative. “Working hard” is weak evidence. The user needs to frame impact, not effort.

**Main issue:**  
The message does not anchor the ask in measurable value.

## Improved version

I’d like to discuss my compensation in light of the impact I’ve delivered over the past [time period], particularly [achievement 1], [achievement 2], and [achievement 3].

I understand budget may be a consideration, so I’d like to discuss what adjustment would be fair based on my current scope, contribution, and market level.

## Why this is stronger

- Leads with value.
- Removes weak language.
- Uses fairness framing.
- Positions the conversation around contribution and scope.
- Avoids sounding emotional or apologetic.

## Stronger option

I’d like to review my compensation based on the level of responsibility and impact I’m currently delivering. My role has expanded across [scope], and the outcomes include [result].

What would need to be true for us to align my compensation with the current level of contribution?

# Example 5: Saying no to extra work

## User text

Sorry, I don’t think I can do this today. I have a lot on, but I can try if it’s really urgent.

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
The user is trying to say no, but leaves the door open to being pressured.

**Power lens:**  
The apology and “I can try” weaken the boundary. The user needs to make the trade-off clear.

**Main issue:**  
The boundary is unclear.

## Improved version

I can take this on, but not today without moving another priority. What would you like me to deprioritise to make room for it?

## Why this is stronger

- Uses a calibrated question.
- Makes the trade-off visible.
- Avoids unnecessary apology.
- Keeps the tone collaborative.
- Protects the user’s time without sounding difficult.

## Stronger option

I cannot take this on today without affecting the current priority. If this is more urgent, we need to decide what moves down the list.

# Example 6: Responding to criticism

## User text

I’m sorry if my design wasn’t clear. I can change it if everyone thinks it is wrong.

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
The user accepts criticism without understanding the real concern. They should ask a calibrated question before conceding.

**Power lens:**  
The message collapses too quickly. The user sounds unsure of their own design judgement.

**Main issue:**  
The user gives away authority before understanding the objection.

## Improved version

It sounds like the main concern is clarity. Before I change the direction, can we align on which part is creating confusion for users: the structure, the visual hierarchy, or the interaction?

## Why this is stronger

- Uses labeling.
- Slows the conversation down.
- Protects the user’s design judgement.
- Turns vague criticism into specific feedback.
- Avoids unnecessary apology.

## Stronger option

I’m open to changing the design, but I want to make sure we solve the right problem. What specifically is unclear: the structure, the hierarchy, or the interaction?

# Example 7: LinkedIn message to a senior person

## User text

Hi, sorry for the random message. I saw your post and thought it was interesting. I’m looking for a job and was wondering if you could help or maybe give me advice.

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
The ask is too broad. The other person has to do too much work to respond.

**Power lens:**  
The user sounds needy. The message does not show enough relevance, value, or professional identity.

**Main issue:**  
The ask is vague and the positioning is weak.

## Improved version

Hi [Name], I saw your post on [topic] and it connects closely with my work in product design and production AI.

I’m currently positioning myself for senior roles where AI, complex workflows, and product execution matter. Would you be opposed to sharing one suggestion on how I should frame that experience more clearly?

## Why this is stronger

- Creates relevance.
- Uses the user’s brand.
- Makes a small ask.
- Uses a no-oriented question.
- Sounds professional instead of needy.

## Stronger option

Hi [Name], your post on [topic] stood out because it connects directly with my work turning AI concepts into production-ready product experiences.

I’m refining my positioning for senior roles in this space. Would you be opposed to sharing one sharp suggestion on how to make that story stronger?

# Example 8: Stakeholder update

## User text

Just a quick update, I’ve been working on the designs and hopefully they are okay. Let me know if you have any feedback.

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
The message does not guide the stakeholder toward useful feedback.

**Power lens:**  
“Hopefully they are okay” weakens confidence. The user should present decisions, not ask for general approval.

**Main issue:**  
The message lacks ownership.

## Improved version

I’ve updated the designs based on the latest feedback, with a focus on improving clarity, reducing decision friction, and making the next action easier for users.

The key changes are:
- [Change 1]
- [Change 2]
- [Change 3]

Could you review the flow and confirm whether this direction is aligned before I finalise the next iteration?

## Why this is stronger

- Shows ownership.
- Frames the work around outcomes.
- Makes feedback easier.
- Gives a clear next step.
- Sounds senior and structured.

## Stronger option

I’ve updated the designs to address the main usability risks: clarity, decision friction, and next-step visibility.

Please review the flow and confirm whether this direction is aligned so I can finalise the next iteration.

# Example 9: Client pushes for lower price

## User text

I understand the budget is low. I can reduce the price if needed.

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
The user gives a concession without getting anything in return.

**Power lens:**  
The user weakens their value by reducing price too quickly.

**Main issue:**  
The user concedes without trading.

## Improved version

I understand budget is a constraint. I can work within a lower budget, but we would need to adjust the scope to protect quality.

Which parts are the highest priority for you?

## Why this is stronger

- Uses tactical empathy.
- Does not give a free concession.
- Trades budget reduction for scope reduction.
- Uses a calibrated question.
- Protects the user’s value.

## Stronger option

I can reduce the scope to fit the budget, but I would not recommend reducing the price for the same level of work. Which outcomes matter most so we can prioritise properly?

# Example 10: Asking for decision clarity

## User text

I’m confused about what we are doing now. Can someone clarify?

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
The need is valid, but the wording may sound frustrated and unstructured.

**Power lens:**  
The user sounds reactive. A stronger version would create alignment and show leadership.

**Main issue:**  
The message identifies confusion but does not lead the group toward a decision.

## Improved version

I want to clarify the decision before we move further. At the moment, there seem to be two possible directions:

1. [Option A]
2. [Option B]

Which direction are we committing to, and who needs to approve it?

## Why this is stronger

- Turns confusion into structure.
- Shows leadership.
- Uses a calibrated question.
- Creates decision clarity.
- Avoids emotional leakage.

## Stronger option

Before we continue, we need to align on the decision. Are we moving forward with [Option A] or [Option B], and who is the final decision-maker?

“I can try” sounds weak and creates risk. The user should define what makes the deadline possible.

**Main issue:**  
The user gives a soft commitment without conditions.

## Why this is stronger

- Shows trade-offs.
- Creates two options.
- Uses a calibrated question.
- Protects credibility.

# Example 15: When the original is already strong

## User text

I recommend we pause this release until the accessibility issue is resolved. The current version creates a risk for users relying on keyboard navigation, and fixing it now will prevent rework after launch.

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**  
This is strong. It clearly states the issue, the risk, and the reason for action.

**Power lens:**  
The user sounds confident, professional, and outcome-focused.

**Main issue:**  
No major issue. The only improvement would be adding a clear decision question.

## Improved version

I recommend we pause this release until the accessibility issue is resolved. The current version creates a risk for users relying on keyboard navigation, and fixing it now will prevent rework after launch.

Can we align today on whether this is a release blocker?

## Why this is stronger

- Keeps the strong recommendation.
- Adds a clear next step.
- Frames the issue as a decision.
- Maintains authority without aggression.

# Example 16: Conflict with a colleague

## User text

## Diagnosis

**Negotiation lens:**

**Main issue:**  
The wording attacks understanding rather than defining the required outcome.

## Improved version

It looks like we may be solving slightly different problems. What I need is [specific outcome], and the current version addresses [different outcome].

Can we realign on the goal before making the next change?

## Why this is stronger

- Uses a label.
- Avoids blame.
- Clarifies the gap.
- Uses a calibrated question.
- Keeps the user in control of the outcome.

## Stronger option

We need to realign on the goal. The current version solves [X], but the requirement is [Y]. Can we correct the direction before more time goes into revisions?

- States the ask clearly.
- Adds evidence.
- Uses fairness framing.
- Keeps the tone controlled.
- Uses a calibrated question.

I’m requesting a partial refund due to the condition of the service provided. The issues were significant enough to affect our experience, especially [specific issue].

## User text

The value proposition is too broad. It does not help the other person understand what to do with the user.

**Power lens:**

**Main issue:**  
The message does not communicate a distinct professional identity.

I build AI that goes live and creates impact. My work focuses on turning complex AI ideas into scalable products with measurable business value, from concept and architecture through to product execution.

## Why this is stronger

- Uses the user’s strongest brand statement.
- Communicates production focus.
- Shows business impact.
- Avoids generic positioning.
- Sounds senior and differentiated.

## Stronger option

My focus is production-ready AI: turning complex AI ideas into real systems that ship, scale, and create measurable business value.
